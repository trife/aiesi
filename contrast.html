Part 1 · ~15 min

Two ways to make a computer "see"

Different approach and the same blind spot.

Hub Materials Demos Teachable Machine Learned vs. Rules
✍️

You wrote the rules by hand

The browser demos. Canopy cover, object counting, greenness index, edge detection - every one of them is a threshold or a formula you set with a slider. Nobody trained anything; you are the model.

How it fails: the "right" threshold doesn't exist - move the slider and the answer changes, with no signal telling you which value was correct. Shadows, clumps, and lighting break hand-written rules just as easily as they break a trained model, just in more predictable ways.

🧠

You trained it by example

Teachable Machine. You showed it labeled leaves - healthy vs. diseased, species A vs. species B - and it found the pattern on its own. Nobody wrote down what "diseased" looks like; the model inferred it from your photos.

How it fails: it learns whatever's actually in the training photos, which isn't always the plant. Lighting, background, camera angle - any of those can become "the pattern" instead of the leaf itself. And it answers confidently even on a blurry photo, a hand, or a leaf it's never seen.

Both need validation. Neither one knows when it's wrong. A trained model gives you a confidence score that doesn't mean what it sounds like it means. A hand-written rule gives you a precise-looking number that's only as good as the threshold behind it. In both cases, the check is the same: does this match what a person looking carefully would say? That's the trust check - and it's the same six questions regardless of which paradigm produced the answer.
